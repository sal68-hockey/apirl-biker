Outlaws. Rebels. Legends.

The 1950s: Origins & Identity

The Hells Angels Motorcycle Club (HAMC) was founded in 1948 in Fontana, California. During the 1950s, the club expanded throughout California, establishing a reputation for defiance and camaraderie. Members were known for riding Harley-Davidson motorcycles, often acquired from police auctions and meticulously rebuilt.

The club's image was shaped by media portrayals, notably the 1947 Hollister riot, which inspired the film The Wild One. This era marked the beginning of the "one-percenter" identity, distinguishing outlaw bikers from the mainstream motorcycling community.

The 1960s: Expansion & Infamy

The 1960s saw the Hells Angels' expansion beyond California, including the establishment of international chapters, such as in New Zealand in 1961. Their notoriety grew due to high-profile incidents and a rebellious image that both fascinated and alarmed the public.

A significant event was the Altamont Free Concert in 1969, where the Hells Angels provided security. The event turned tragic with the death of a concertgoer, highlighting the club's volatile reputation.
