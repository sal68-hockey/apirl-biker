The Hells Angels in the 60s & 70s

Legendary outlaws. Rebel culture. A biker brotherhood that shook the world.

Who They Were

From Jesse James and Butch Cassidy to Scarface and Tony Soprano, outlaws have held an ambiguous place in America’s popular imagination , The Hells Angels are no exception, formed in 1948 in California. By the 60s, they were an infamous outlaw motorcycle club built on brotherhood, rebellion, and freedom. Members lived by their own rules and often rejected mainstream society.

What They Did

Known for wild rides and parties, the Angels also gained notoriety at the 1969 Altamont concert. Accusations ranged from drug and weapon trafficking to violent crime, though members saw themselves as misunderstood freedom-seekers.

Fashion

Angels wore leather jackets, denim cuts, and the iconic "death head" patch. Their fashion wasn't just about looks—it was about loyalty, identity, and fearlessness. Their style influenced rockers, punks, and rebels everywhere.

Cultural Impact

From books like Hunter S. Thompson's *Hell’s Angels* to films like *Easy Rider*, the club became a symbol of anti-establishment rebellion. Their influence is still seen in fashion, music, and outsider culture today.

The Open Road

Endless highways. Brotherhood. Freedom. The Hells Angels legacy lives on.

Timeline: 1960s–1970s

  • 1961: Oakland chapter opens under Sonny Barger.
  • 1965: Featured in LIFE magazine.
  • 1967: Hunter S. Thompson publishes Hell’s Angels.
  • 1969: Altamont Free Concert ends in violence.
  • 1971: London chapter opens—first international expansion.
  • 1973: Hells Angels feature in underground biker films.
  • 1975: FBI begins formal surveillance and tracking.
  • 1977: Australian and Canadian chapters launched.
  • 1979: Nationwide raids conducted in U.S. cities.
