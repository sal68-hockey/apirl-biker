Hells Angels in the 1960s

Who They Were

The Hells Angels Motorcycle Club, formed in the late 1940s, became iconic in the 1960s as an outlaw biker group known for their rebellious lifestyle, close brotherhood, and anti-establishment attitude.

What They Did

Throughout the 60s, the Hells Angels gained notoriety for their rowdy rides, parties, clashes with law enforcement, and their presence at events like the Rolling Stones concert at Altamont in 1969. They lived on the edge of the law, often accused of violence and criminal activity.

The Clothes They Wore

They wore leather jackets, denim vests (called “cuts”), patches with skulls and flames, heavy boots, and bandanas. Their clothing symbolized their loyalty and defiance, with the iconic winged skull logo on the back.

How They Changed the World

The Hells Angels influenced pop culture, music, and the image of the "outlaw biker." They were both feared and idolized. Their style and attitude inspired films, books, and a lasting fascination with biker subculture.

1960s & 1970s Timeline

April 1961

Hells Angels expand to Oakland under Sonny Barger, increasing their notoriety and influence.

March 1965

Featured in LIFE magazine, Hells Angels gain national attention, solidifying their place in American culture.

Summer 1966

Hells Angels are banned from California's beaches due to violent clashes with locals, marking a shift in their public image.

April 1967

Hunter S. Thompson publishes Hell’s Angels: The Strange and Terrible Saga of the Outlaw Motorcycle Gangs, offering an inside look into the club.

December 6, 1969

Hells Angels are hired as security for the Rolling Stones' Altamont Free Concert, where violence erupts, forever linking the group to the infamous event.

End of 1969

After Altamont, Hells Angels continue to grow in number, establishing chapters across the U.S. and even internationally.

1971

Hells Angels continue their expansion in Europe, opening chapters in London and other parts of the UK, bringing their outlaw culture to a global audience.

1972

Sonny Barger becomes the public face of the Hells Angels, further solidifying his legendary status as the club's leader and spokesperson.

1973

The Hells Angels gain notoriety in the press for their involvement in violent confrontations with rival gangs and law enforcement, making them a feared group nationwide.

1975

The Hells Angels are featured in the film Hells Angels on Wheels, which helps cement their image in pop culture as the ultimate outlaw bikers.

1976

The Hells Angels officially form alliances with several outlaw motorcycle gangs across the world, increasing their influence and establishing a network of chapters globally.
